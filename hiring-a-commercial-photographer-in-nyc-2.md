# Hiring a Commercial Photographer in NYC: What Brands Should Know Before Booking

<div class="kj-intro">

Hiring a commercial photographer is not just about finding someone with a camera and a good-looking portfolio.

For a company, agency, brand manager, designer, entrepreneur, or marketing team, a commercial photoshoot is part of a larger business decision. The images may be used on a website, in advertising, on LinkedIn, in a campaign, in a press release, in an investor presentation, in social media, or across years of brand communication.

That means the photographer has to understand more than exposure and composition. They need to understand lighting, production, people, timing, usage rights, retouching, and how the final images will actually be used.

## What Is a Commercial Photographer?

A commercial photographer creates images for business use.

That can include corporate headshots, executive portraits, advertising images, product photography, fashion campaigns, beauty photography, editorial-style brand images, event visuals, studio productions, and content for websites or marketing campaigns.

The difference between commercial photography and personal photography is purpose.

A personal portrait may be created for memory or personal use. A commercial image has to do a job. It has to support a brand, sell a product, build trust, explain a service, promote a person, or communicate a company’s identity.

The best commercial photography looks polished, but it also feels intentional. It should be created with the final use in mind.

</div>

## Commercial Photography vs. Editorial Photography

Commercial and editorial photography often overlap, but they are not the same.

**Commercial photography** is usually tied to a business goal. The image may need to sell a product, introduce a team, support a campaign, promote a service, or strengthen a brand’s public image.

**Editorial photography** is usually more story-driven. It may be used in magazines, features, lookbooks, creative projects, or visual essays. It often has more room for atmosphere, mood, narrative, and interpretation.

Many strong brand images use both approaches.

A fashion campaign may need the clarity of commercial photography but the emotion of an editorial. A corporate portrait may need to feel professional but still have personality. A product image may need to show the item clearly while also creating desire.

Before hiring a photographer, it helps to understand what your project needs. Are you trying to sell something directly? Build brand trust? Tell a story? Launch a product? Present leadership? Create content for a campaign?

The answer will shape the entire shoot.

## Look Beyond a Pretty Portfolio

A strong portfolio should show more than nice pictures.

When you review a photographer’s work, look for consistency. Can they handle different types of subjects? Do the images feel controlled? Is the lighting intentional? Do the people look comfortable? Does the retouching feel polished but natural? Can the photographer move between studio work and location work?

A single great image can happen by chance. A consistent body of work usually comes from experience.

For corporate photography, look for expression, credibility, lighting, posture, and polish.

For fashion and beauty photography, look for styling awareness, model direction, hair, makeup, movement, texture, and mood.

For product photography, look for shape, surface quality, accurate color, controlled highlights, and attention to detail.

For video production, look for lighting, sound, pacing, b-roll, interview quality, and whether the final piece tells a clear story.

The right photographer should have work that connects to the kind of images you need.

## Lighting Is One of the Biggest Differences

Lighting is often the difference between a professional image and an average one.

Good lighting shapes the face, creates dimension, controls reflections, separates the subject from the background, and helps the image feel intentional. Poor lighting can make even a good subject, product, or location look flat.

In corporate photography, lighting helps someone look confident, approachable, and credible.

In fashion photography, lighting creates mood, movement, and shape.

In beauty photography, lighting affects skin texture, makeup, hair, and product finish.

In product photography, lighting controls surface, color, reflection, and perceived value.

In video, lighting helps the person on camera look professional and present.

A photographer should be able to explain how they approach lighting for your specific project. A headshot, a skincare campaign, a fashion editorial, a corporate interview video, and a reflective product all require different lighting decisions.

</div>

## Production Planning Matters

A commercial shoot has a lot of moving parts.

Depending on the project, the production may involve a photographer, assistant, digital technician, makeup artist, hair stylist, wardrobe stylist, model, producer, retoucher, sound person, video crew, client team, or agency team.

Even a smaller shoot needs planning.

Before the shoot, it helps to define:

- What the images are for
- How many final images are needed
- Where the images will be used
- Whether the shoot is in studio or on location
- Whether talent, models, hair, makeup, or styling are needed
- What backgrounds or locations are required
- What mood or style the brand wants
- What usage rights are needed
- What the deadline is
- What level of retouching is expected

The more clearly those details are discussed before the shoot, the smoother the production will be.

A professional photographer should help you think through these questions. The goal is not to make the process complicated. The goal is to avoid surprises.

</div>

## Studio vs. Location

Some projects are best photographed in a studio. Others need to happen on location.

A studio gives you control. Lighting, background, equipment, makeup, wardrobe, tethered review, and schedule can all be managed in one place. This is useful for headshots, product photography, beauty images, fashion tests, small video productions, and controlled campaign work.

A location adds context. It can show an office, workspace, city environment, architecture, lifestyle, event space, or brand setting. Location work can create a stronger story, but it also comes with more logistics.

In New York City, location work can involve building access, elevators, freight, security, certificates of insurance, parking, loading, power, noise, and time restrictions.

A beautiful location is not enough. The photographer has to know how to work with the space and still create a polished result.

## Retouching Should Support the Image

Retouching is part of professional commercial photography, but it should be handled with taste.

The goal is usually to polish the image, not make it look fake.

For corporate portraits, retouching may include skin cleanup, stray hair removal, shine control, wardrobe cleanup, and background cleanup.

For beauty images, retouching may be more detailed, but skin should still look like skin.

For product images, retouching may involve dust removal, color correction, reflection control, and surface cleanup.

For fashion images, retouching should support the styling, model, garment, and mood of the story.

Good retouching should not call attention to itself. It should make the image feel finished.

## Understand Usage Rights and Licensing

Commercial photography is not just about creating images. It is also about how those images can be used.

Before booking a shoot, discuss usage.

Will the images be used on a website? LinkedIn? Social media? Paid advertising? Print materials? Press releases? Packaging? Trade show displays? A national campaign? Internal communications? For how long? In what territories?

Usage matters because commercial images have value beyond the day they are created.

In most professional photography arrangements, the photographer retains copyright and the client licenses specific usage rights. A full copyright buyout is usually a separate and more expensive arrangement. Work made for hire is also a specific legal category and should be clearly agreed to in writing when applicable.

This is why a clear estimate or agreement matters. It protects both sides and prevents confusion later.

A good licensing agreement should explain what is included, how the images may be used, and what would require additional permission or fees.

</div>

## Why Experience Matters

Commercial shoots often require quick decisions.

A location may not look the way it did in photos. A subject may be uncomfortable in front of the camera. A product may reflect everything in the room. A conference room may have terrible lighting. A schedule may change. A client may ask for a new direction during the shoot.

Experience helps solve those problems without losing the purpose of the project.

After more than 30 years in photography, I have learned that a strong shoot depends on preparation, but it also depends on staying flexible. The plan matters, but so does the ability to adjust.

That is especially true in New York City, where every production has its own set of practical issues.

## Frequently Asked Questions

<div class="kj-faq">

### How much does it cost to hire a commercial photographer in NYC?

Commercial photography pricing depends on the scope of the project. The cost may be affected by the number of shoot days, usage rights, location, crew, equipment, retouching, number of final images, and production complexity. A corporate headshot session, product shoot, fashion editorial, and advertising campaign will all be priced differently.

### What is the difference between commercial and corporate photography?

Corporate photography is one type of commercial photography. It usually includes headshots, executive portraits, team photos, office images, and business branding photography. Commercial photography is broader and can include fashion, beauty, product, advertising, lifestyle, editorial, and video production.

### Do I own the photos after a commercial shoot?

Usually, the photographer retains copyright and the client receives a license to use the images for specific agreed purposes. A full copyright transfer or buyout is a separate agreement. Usage rights should be discussed before the shoot.

### How long does a commercial photography session take?

It depends on the project. A single headshot may take a short session, while a larger team shoot, fashion campaign, product shoot, or video production may require a half day, full day, or multiple production days.

### What should I look for in a commercial photography portfolio?

Look for consistency, lighting quality, technical control, experience with similar subjects, natural direction of people, polished retouching, and images that align with your brand’s needs.

### Can AI replace a commercial photographer?

AI can be useful for ideas, references, or certain visual experiments, but it does not replace a real production when a brand needs authentic people, real products, accurate lighting, controlled direction, exclusive images, and a photographer who can manage the shoot and solve problems on set.

### Does Ken Jones Photography offer on-location shoots outside Manhattan?

Yes. Ken Jones Photography works in studio and on location throughout New York City, including Manhattan, Brooklyn, Queens, and surrounding areas.

### What is included in professional retouching?

Retouching may include color correction, skin cleanup, stray hair removal, shine control, clothing cleanup, background cleanup, product cleanup, and final polish. The exact level depends on the project and intended use of the images.
